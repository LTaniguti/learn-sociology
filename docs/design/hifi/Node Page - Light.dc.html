Social Facts

Intermediate
Functionalism

Durkheim's foundational claim: society exerts real, external constraint on individuals — patterns of behavior that exist independent of any one person's will.

A social fact, for Durkheim, is any way of acting — fixed or not — capable of exercising external constraint over the individual. Ways of feeling and acting, external to the individual, endowed with coercive power by virtue of which they control them.

This is the methodological pivot of The Rules of Sociological Method (1895): sociology should treat social facts as things, studied through observable, external evidence rather than individual psychology.

Perspectives

Functionalism
Social facts persist because they perform a function for social cohesion — order is explained by what holds a system together.
Conflict
Constraint isn't neutral — social facts often encode whose interests are protected, and who bears the cost of "order."
Interactionism
Even "external" facts are reproduced moment to moment through everyday interaction — constraint is enacted, not just inherited.
