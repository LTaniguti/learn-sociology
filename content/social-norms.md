## Definition

Social norms are the shared expectations that define appropriate behavior within a group or society. They translate a culture's abstract values into concrete rules for everyday conduct — how to dress, speak, queue, eat, grieve, and celebrate. Norms are mostly invisible while followed and become visible mainly when broken.

## In depth

If values are what a culture considers good, norms are the instructions for acting on it. A society that values education (a value) produces expectations of school attendance, homework completion, and respect for teachers (norms). This dependence runs one way: norms are derived from values, which is why this node lists `values-and-beliefs` as a prerequisite.

Sociologists since William Graham Sumner have sorted norms by their moral weight and mode of enforcement:

- **Folkways** are norms of routine and convention — covering your mouth when yawning, facing forward in an elevator. Violations draw mild reactions: a strange look, a joke, quiet judgment.
- **Mores** (pronounced *MOR-ays*) carry moral significance. They embody a group's ethical sense — honesty, loyalty, prohibitions on cruelty. Violating mores provokes serious condemnation and can end relationships or careers.
- **Laws** are norms formalized by the state and enforced through official sanctions. Many laws codify existing mores (prohibitions on theft or assault), though law and mores can drift apart — some acts are legal but widely condemned, others illegal but socially tolerated.

Two further distinctions matter. Norms can be **prescriptive** (do this: tip your server) or **proscriptive** (don't do this: don't cut in line). And they can be **formal** — written down, officially enforced — or **informal**, learned by observation and enforced through everyday social reactions. Most of social life runs on informal norms; no statute requires you to say "thank you," yet nearly everyone does.

Enforcement operates through **sanctions**: rewards for conformity (praise, inclusion, promotion) and penalties for violation (ridicule, exclusion, punishment). Because most sanctions are informal and most conformity is habitual, norms usually feel less like rules and more like "just how things are done" — which is precisely what makes them powerful.

Norms are also relative. They vary across cultures, across subcultures within one society, across situations (shouting is expected at a stadium, deviant at a funeral), and across time — smoking indoors moved from folkway-compliant to more-violating within a generation.

## Perspectives

- **Functionalist view.** Norms are the connective tissue of social order. Shared expectations make behavior predictable, coordinate strangers, and hold society together; even the punishment of violations reaffirms the group's boundaries.
- **Conflict view.** Norms are not neutral. Dominant groups have disproportionate power to define which behaviors count as normal, and norms often protect existing hierarchies — historical etiquette systems governing class, race, and gender deference are norms doing the work of inequality.
- **Interactionist view.** Norms exist only insofar as people continually enact and negotiate them in interaction. They are learned, tested, bent, and remade in everyday encounters, which explains both their persistence and their capacity to change.

## Examples

- **Queueing.** Standing in line is an informal, prescriptive folkway in many societies. Cutting in line triggers immediate informal sanctions — glares, complaints — with no law involved.
- **Plagiarism.** In academic communities, presenting another's work as your own violates a more, not merely a folkway; sanctions (expulsion, retraction, reputational ruin) are severe even where no law is broken.
- **Smoking indoors.** A norm that migrated across all three categories in living memory: from accepted folkway, to violation of mores around health and courtesy, to formal law in many jurisdictions.
