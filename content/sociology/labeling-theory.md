## Definition

Labeling theory argues that no act is deviant in itself; deviance is created when social groups define certain behaviors as deviant and successfully attach that label to particular people. The theory's focus is therefore not on why people break rules but on who gets labeled a rule-breaker, by whom, and with what consequences — because the label, once applied, can reorganize a person's opportunities, relationships, and self-concept.

## In depth

Labeling theory grows directly out of symbolic interactionism, which is why `symbolic-interactionism` is a prerequisite even though this node sits under `deviance` in the concept tree. If meaning arises through interaction, then "deviant" is a meaning like any other: assigned, negotiated, and consequential.

Howard Becker's formulation is the theory's touchstone: social groups create deviance by making the rules whose violation constitutes deviance, and by applying those rules to particular people. Two implications follow. First, the same act can be deviant in one context and unremarkable in another — the act hasn't changed; the audience and its response have. Second, labeling is selective. Not everyone who breaks a rule is labeled, and some are labeled without breaking rules at all. Who gets caught, prosecuted, diagnosed, or expelled tracks social position — age, race, class, gender, and reputation — as much as behavior.

Edwin Lemert sharpened the theory with the distinction between two stages of deviance:

- **Primary deviance** is the initial rule-breaking act — often minor, situational, and without lasting effect on identity. Most people commit primary deviance (speeding, underage drinking, petty theft in youth) and simply move on.
- **Secondary deviance** occurs when a person, having been labeled, begins to organize their behavior and identity around the label. The societal reaction, not the original act, drives this stage: once treated as "a delinquent" or "an addict," a person may be excluded from conventional opportunities, pushed toward deviant associates, and come to accept the label as self-definition.

This escalation often works through the **self-fulfilling prophecy**: the label restricts legitimate options (employment, housing, trust), making continued deviance more likely, which then appears to confirm the original label. When a label becomes the primary lens through which others see a person — overriding jobs, family roles, and every other identity — sociologists call it a **master status**. "Ex-convict" and "felon" function this way; the stigma persists long after any sentence ends, shaping access to work, voting, and housing.

Labeling theory redirected the study of deviance from deviants to **the audiences and institutions that label**: police, courts, schools, psychiatry, media. It underpins real policy debates — juvenile-record sealing, diversion programs, and "person-first" language all rest on the premise that labels have causal power.

The theory has well-known limits. It explains the amplification of deviance better than its origins (why the first act occurred at all), and critics note that some serious harms are condemned near-universally, labeled or not. Strain theory (see `strain-theory`) fills part of this gap by locating the roots of deviance in blocked structural opportunity.

## Perspectives

As a theory that belongs to the interactionist paradigm, this node's Perspectives section works differently: rather than three readings of a neutral concept, it records how the other paradigms respond to the theory itself.

- **Functionalist response.** Durkheim's insight that deviance is normal and boundary-defining is compatible with labeling theory, but functionalists object that labeling cannot explain rule-breaking that occurs before any societal reaction, and that some acts threaten social order regardless of labels.
- **Conflict extension.** Conflict theorists embrace and radicalize the theory's selectivity claim: labeling is not merely uneven but systematically serves powerful groups, criminalizing the behaviors of the poor while normalizing comparable harms committed by elites (compare street crime with wage theft or corporate pollution).

## Examples

- **The schoolyard fight.** Two students fight; one is suspended and referred to police, the other receives detention. Identical primary deviance, divergent labels — and research on school discipline shows such divergence patterns by race and class, feeding the "school-to-prison pipeline."
- **Criminal records and employment.** Audit studies in which matched applicants differ only in a disclosed criminal record show large drops in employer callbacks — the label alone, independent of any observed behavior, closes doors and raises the odds of secondary deviance.
- **Medicalized labels.** A diagnosis can operate as a label with both protective effects (access to treatment, reduced blame) and stigmatizing ones (assumptions of incompetence or danger), illustrating that labeling is not inherently punitive but always consequential.
